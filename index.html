Zoning for what’s next:
drones, robo-fleets & EV charging

Mesa is proposing updates to its Zoning Ordinance so the code can keep pace with drone delivery, robo-taxi and delivery fleets, and the growth of private-sector EV charging. The goal is to allow these uses where they fit, with standards that protect nearby homes and businesses. Explore what’s being proposed, and see exactly where on the interactive map.

The big picture

What Mesa is doing, and why

Mesa’s zoning code was written before today’s mobility and energy technologies were common. In places it doesn’t clearly accommodate newer uses like commercial EV charging, private vehicle fleets, and aerial (drone) delivery. The proposed text amendments would add clear definitions, say where each use belongs, and set modern development standards, amending Title 11, Chapters 6, 7, 8, 31 & 86. They are a starting point for discussion and may change with public and Council feedback.

New & revised use

Fleet-Based Services

A clear new framework for sites that park, stage, dispatch, fuel and charge a fleet, ground or aerial. Split into Light (≤24 vehicles <10k lbs) and a brand-new Heavy tier.

New accessory use

Accessory EV Charging

Lets a normal business add up to 16 charging spaces without becoming a service station, and without taking the parking it’s required to provide.

Modernized use

Service Stations

The definition now includes dispensing electricity via EVSE, serves the public (not fleets), and gets updated canopy, lighting, stacking & neighborhood-separation standards.

Emerging technology

Drone Delivery

Aerial delivery (Zipline, Wing, Amazon Prime Air) is regulated as an aerial Fleet-Based Service, with screening and height rules tailored to launch-and-recover yards.
